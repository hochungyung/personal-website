McGill University: Pros and Cons from a Anglophone Undergrad

5/25/2020

I was recently asked a question from my sister's friend who was deciding between attending NYU and McGill, how was your experience going to McGill and living in Montreal? This prompted me to write this post on what I felt are the pros and cons of attending McGill University from my perspective.

To give some background about myself, I'm a recent graduate of the School of Arts. I did a double Major in Software Engineering and Economics, taking classes from 2015 to 2020.

The Pros

1. Campus and Location

One of the unique parts about McGill is the school has a beautiful and spacious main campus, despite being in the heart of downtown Montreal. Downtown across the street from the main entrance (Roddick Gates), giving the students easy access to the metro (subway) systems and other shops on Rue St. Catherine.

The university is also located at the foot of a small mountain, Mount Royal, which surrounds the school with it's various parks and running trails. Saint-Laurent Boulevard, where a lot of the city's clubs and bars are located, is only a 10-15 minute walk from campus - a fact you will be grateful for at 5am in the morning.

2. Social Scene

McGill as a school embodies the mentality of work hard play hard. You can hear students going out every day of the week, especially early in the semester. Despite this, you can also find the same students in the library next morning at 9am because they have a midterm coming up. The school does not throw any big parties or tail-gates, but the nature of Montreal and the people around you lead to positive social scene.

3. Drinking Age and Legalization

One of the biggest pros about Montreal was the fact that 18 year olds could purchase alcohol, and more recently cannabis, freely. Coming from the US this was a great quality of life change that I began to take for granted. Laws in Quebec do impose certain restrictions, like only being able to purchase liquor at government owned SAQ stores and cannabis SQDC stores, but the pros outweigth the cons.

4. Student City and Cheap Rent

Montreal is the home to four major universities (McGill, Concordia, UQAM, UDEM), which in turn make it student friendly. Rent is very cheap compared to cities like Toronto and New York and it only gets cheaper the further you get from downtown. I was paying on average $650 CAD per month living with two other roommates, living 20 minutes away from campus.

Your neighbors are likely to be other students too who study at one of the Montreal schools. It's easy to meet new people and grow your social circle

5. Diversity

McGill and Montreal are diverse in demographic and I have gotten to meet tons of people I otherwise would have never met. Francophones are the majority of the population, but the city is relatively English friendly and the student body is diverse in both background and interest.

6. Cost

Tuition for McGill is relatively cheap for international students and extremely cheap for Canadian or Quebec residents. I was paying less than $5000 CAD for one year at McGill, which is insane when compared to schools in the US.

The Cons

1. Student Support

McGill provides a poor support network for it's students in terms of academics and health. The university largely leaves students to fend for themselves. It can be a jarring change coming from high school and students are forced to adapt quickly, or otherwise fail their courses.

2. Academic Focused

McGill is a research focused school and as a result, less emphasis is placed on the well-being of undergraduate students. This also leads the university to place heavy emphasis on academic rigour, to the annoyance of the student body. This isn't all bad as it breeds displcine and knowledge, however, certain infamous Computer Science courses tested my existence as a human being

3. Language Barrier

While McGill is an English speaking university, the rest of Montreal is not and it can be frustrating for anglophone students to deal with. The majority of Montreal population speaks English, but it's on varying degrees of proficiency and some people are rude when you only speak English. I can fondly recall moments of absurdity and disrespect from people when they found out I couldn't speak French.

4. Recruiting

There is not a lot of emphasis being placed on recruiting in general. Don't expect the school to offer great assistance in helping undergraduate students find internships or jobs outside of the yearly job fair. Networking and speaking events take place around the year, but are not emphasized by the school.

Management and Engineering faculties are better in this respect, however, other faculites are left out. This is one of the downsides of being a research oriented school.
